Taqi Iftikhar Theory:
My father, a retired university administrator, once shared a tale that has stuck with me ever since. It was a story about the value of experience versus education, and it involved a character he named Taghi Iftikhar.
Taghi was an old-school telecommunications technician at the university. Despite having little formal education, he knew the university's systems inside and out. When the time came to replace him, they hired a bright young graduate who had all the latest degrees.
The new hire was eager to prove himself, but trouble seemed to follow him wherever he went. Wires got crossed, systems crashed, and no matter how hard he tried, he couldn’t seem to get anything to work. It wasn’t long before they had to call Taghi back in to fix the countless problems the new employee had created.
At first, everyone thought it was just a series of unfortunate incidents. But as time went on, a pattern emerged. Whenever there was a major event or project, something would go wrong, and Taghi would be the one called in to save the day.
One day, a colleague of mine noticed something odd. It seemed like Taghi was almost too good at his job. He could anticipate problems before they even happened. Intrigued, my colleague began to investigate.
What he discovered was shocking. Taghi had been deliberately sabotaging the system to make himself look indispensable. He would create problems, then swoop in to solve them, earning the admiration of his colleagues and superiors.
My father used this story to illustrate a valuable lesson: sometimes, those who appear to be the most knowledgeable or experienced are actually the ones causing the most trouble. He coined the term "Taqi Iftikhar Theory" to describe this phenomenon, and it has served as a cautionary tale ever since.
While I don't believe that everyone who seems to know what they're doing is secretly trying to sabotage things, this story has taught me to be more skeptical and to always question the motives of others. It's a reminder that appearances can be deceiving, and that sometimes the simplest explanation is the most likely one.
